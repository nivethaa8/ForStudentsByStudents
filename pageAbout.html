About Problem Solving and Decision Making

    Organizations, people, products and services DO things. In life, in this course and in your overall program, you will solve many problems and make many decisions about:

      What to do.

      What it should look like.

      How to do it.

      Who should do it.

      When it it should be done.

      Where it should be done.

      How it should be evaluated, assessed.

      Etc.

Skills and Skills Development

    Problem solving and decision making are cognitive skills. A skill is the “ability to do something well” (Oxford Dictionary). From Cognitive Science, we know that skills do not improve much unless there is deliberate practice with feedback. Doing something for years and years does not mean it will be improved. Lots of self-reflection, feedback, and structure are needed to improve skills.

    For each of these areas, we evaluate what “should” be done - knowing that “should” depends on the situation. The tools and methods to figure out the “should” are derived from information systems (the technology), organizational behavior (the people), and operations research (the math). During the next five years, you will learn many tools and methods to help you “do what SHOULD be done”. However, you must also learn to understand the situation and pick the appropriate approach, tool and method.

    Expert professionals do not rely on luck. Repeated success relies on skills like:

      Identifying a problem, requirement or opportunity

      Understanding the problem, second-level issues and technical requirements

      Evaluating a problem, how big it is what it implies and if it should be dealt with or not

      Figuring out an appropriate level of efficiency and effectiveness

      Deriving or figuring out possible solutions

      Evaluating alternatives

      Designing and building (or otherwise procuring) the solution

      Deploying the solution, marketing, supporting it

      And many more

    Over time and with deliberate practice it is possible for an individual to develop many of these skills, but it is rare for one individual to have all the skills needed. Very rare. In the best organizations, you have the right people with the right skills doing the right things. This requires another skill - teamwork.

Example: Business Applications

    To perform highly in a business situation, you will eventually need to master three knowledge sources:

      The specific mechanics and tools appropriate to the situation (e.g., project management methods, optimization methods, supply chain management, user interface design).

      The general domain in which you are making decisions (e.g., automotive supply chain, metal forming, healthcare, hospitals, finance, online retail, mobile apps).

      The specific context where it will all come together - the specific users, this factory, this hospital, this business, this product, this service.
