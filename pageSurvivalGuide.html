Real World Problem Solving

Often in a long career, it is not uncommon to be part of an effort to introduce a significant innovation, or help with the supervision or management of one after a year or two. The following are thoughts to consider in either case:

    1. NEVER forget the people. You need to remember them - especially if you want to have the people do things differently or use the innovation. You need to respect them and understand the present, the past - as well as where things are going. This is a whole topic to learn about with its own skills, transition methods, and processes.

    2. General Steps for Problem-solving:

      i) ‘why do it’, ‘what is the problem’

      ii) ‘what needs to be done’

      iii) ‘figuring out how it needs to be done’

      iv) ‘actually doing it’

      v) ‘doing it repeatedly’, and

      vi) ‘reducing variance’

    After vi), you can then consider vii) reducing ‘waste’ and making it efficient. That is, focus first on effectiveness and then on efficiency. Taking shortcuts rarely results in a long-term success.

    3. General Steps for Problem-solving (when coming into an existing situation):

      i) Take time understanding the history behind the 1st six steps listed in the general steps for problem-solving

      ii) Understand implicit and explicit assumptions and biases

    4. Standardizing Processes:

    When evaluating a process, standardizing, looking for variance, or are looking to “mistake proof”, consider how you are doing it and what you are delivering

    Before standardizing, go through the first 6 steps in the general steps.

    Be careful about the cause and effect relationships

    When standardizing, ensure you include how the standard can be adapted and changed as required

    5. Recognize the possibilities of early life failure and be ready to deal with issues. The more ‘new’ things you are doing, the more opportunities will exist for unpleasant surprises. Rest assured, there will be surprises.

    6. If the situation has inherent variability and uncertainty that cannot be reduced by the process, account for this and for ‘reasonable judgement’ in interpreting the standard and process based on context.

    If you are suggesting that changes be made or that new processes be adopted, do them yourself first if you can. Demonstrate that you know what you are talking about, that you understand the strengths and weaknesses of which you speak. Show the personnel that even you can do the task and get results (or at least you know what they have to do, even if you do not have the skill to do it as well as they can).

"Well, if it can be thought, it can be done, a problem can be overcome."
